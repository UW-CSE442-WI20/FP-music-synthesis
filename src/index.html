Sound Waves

In physics, we visualize sound as waves. When an object moves, the air particles around it vibrate by bouncing off of each other and cause sound waves that move back and forth, which we interpret as sound once the vibrations reach our ears. We represent these waves as pressure that changes over time.

The different parts of a wave can be broken into four parts.
First there is the amplititude, or the height of the wave.
Next, is the wavelength and the period of a wave. The wavelength the horizontal distance of one complete cycle of the wave. This time that it takes for a single completeed cycle is called the period.
Lastly is the frequency. This measures how many cycles pass a specific point in one second.

Pitch

Pitch determines how high or how low a wave sounds. When a sound wave repeats a pattern, it produces a consistent pitch. When these patterns are shortened, the pitch increases. This is also known as increasing the frequency.

Thus, the greater the frequency (or the closer to wavelengths are!), the higher the pitch!
Similarly, the lower the frequency, the lower the pitch.

Oscillators

We use oscillators to generate different wave shapes. These shapes change the quality of the sound, also known as the timbre.

Select different oscillators and try them on the keyboard above.


Sounds are actually comprised of many different frequencies, which are called partial tones. The fundamental frequency is the lowest frequency heard, and is the pure tone of a single frequency. A harmonic is an overtone that is higher than the fundamental frequency.

The sine wave has the most basic tone. It has no partial tones and is made of only the fundamental frequency.
The square wave contains harmonics that are odd-number multiples of the fundamental frequency (300 Hz, 500 Hz, etc).
The sawtooth wave is the harshest sounding oscillator and contains all of the harmonics, not just the odd harmonics!
The triangle wave contains the same harmonics as the square wave, but is twice as low and tapers off as it gets further from the frequency rather than imitating the square wave completely.

Envelopes

An envelope describes how volume changes over time.

There are a few parameters that define an envelope. Try changing them below and listen to how they change the tones played on the keyboard.

Attack

This controls how long it takes for a tone to reach full volume.

Decay

This controls how long it takes for a tone to decrease to the sustain volume.

Sustain

This controls the volume a tone drops to before being released.

Release

This controls how long the volume takes to fade out after a tone is released.
