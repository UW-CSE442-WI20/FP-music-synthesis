Sound Waves

In physics, we visualize sound as waves. When an object moves, the air particles around it vibrate by bouncing off of each other and cause sound waves that move back and forth, which we interpret as sound once the vibrations reach our ears. We represent these waves as pressure that changes over time.

A wave can be broken into four parts:

  • The amplititude, or the height of the wave.
  • The wavelength, or the horizontal distance of one complete cycle of the wave.
  • The period, or how long it takes for a single cycle to completed.
  • The frequency, or how many cycles pass a specific point in one second.

Pitch

Pitch determines how high or how low a wave sounds. When a sound wave repeats a pattern, it produces a consistent pitch. When the frequency is increased (i.e. when wavelengths get closer), the pitch increases.

Envelopes

An envelope describes how volume changes over time.

There are a few parameters that define an envelope. Try changing them below and listen to how they change the volume of the notes played on the keyboard as you hold and release them.

Attack

This controls how long it takes for a tone to reach full volume.

Decay

This controls how long it takes for a tone to decrease to the sustain volume.

Sustain

This controls the volume a tone drops to before being released.

Release

This controls how long the volume takes to fade out after a tone is released.
